Frequently Asked Questions

Malassezia is a genus of yeast that naturally lives on human skin. When it overgrows, it can cause fungal acne (Malassezia folliculitis), seborrheic dermatitis, dandruff, and tinea versicolor. Many common skincare ingredients contain fatty acids with carbon chains of C12–C24 that feed this yeast, making conditions worse. This tool helps you identify those ingredients.

Use this free ingredient checker tool. Paste your product's ingredient list, scan the label with your camera, or search our database of 77+ popular products including CeraVe, Vanicream, The Ordinary, La Roche-Posay, and more. The tool instantly flags every Malassezia-feeding ingredient and suggests safe alternatives. Learn more in our Malassezia Handbook.

Avoid ingredients with fatty acid chains of C12–C24. This includes most plant oils (coconut oil, olive oil, argan oil, jojoba oil, rosehip oil), fatty acids (lauric acid, stearic acid, oleic acid), esters like isopropyl myristate and glyceryl stearate, polysorbates 20/40/60/80, and fermented ingredients like galactomyces ferment filtrate. Safe alternatives include squalane, MCT oil (C8/C10 only), mineral oil, dimethicone, and caprylic/capric triglyceride.

Look for moisturizers free of fatty acids C12–C24 and plant oils. Safe options include products based on squalane, dimethicone, petrolatum, or caprylic/capric triglyceride. Popular seb derm-safe moisturizers include Vanicream products, Sebamed Clear Face Care Gel, and The Ordinary Natural Moisturizing Factors. Use our checker to verify any product.

Tinea versicolor is caused by the same Malassezia yeast as fungal acne and seb derm. Safe ingredients include glycerin, hyaluronic acid, niacinamide, squalane, dimethicone, zinc pyrithione, ketoconazole, selenium sulfide, salicylic acid, and azelaic acid. Avoid coconut oil, olive oil, shea butter, and other plant oils with C12–C24 fatty acids.

Fatty alcohols like cetyl alcohol (C16), stearyl alcohol (C18), and cetearyl alcohol are debated in the fungal acne community. They are generally better tolerated than oils and esters, but some people with Malassezia-prone skin still react to them. Our tool marks these as "caution" rather than "unsafe." If you're unsure, patch-test first.

Look for mineral sunscreens with zinc oxide or titanium dioxide as active ingredients, and avoid those with plant oils or fatty acid esters in the inactive ingredients. EltaMD UV Clear SPF 46 and CeraVe Mineral Body Sunscreen SPF 50 are popular choices. Always check the full ingredient list with our tool since many sunscreens contain hidden Malassezia-feeding ingredients.
